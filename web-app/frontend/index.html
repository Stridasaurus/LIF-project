About the Leaky Integrate-and-Fire Model

The Leaky Integrate-and-Fire (LIF) neuron, first described by Louis Lapicque in 1907, is the foundational model of computational neuroscience. It reduces the complex electrochemistry of a biological neuron to two ideas: passive membrane decay (the "leaky" part) and threshold-triggered spike emission (the "fire" part).

τm · dV/dt  =  −(V − Vrest) + R(t) · I(t)

When V(t) reaches the threshold Vthr(t), a spike is recorded and the membrane potential is reset to Vreset. The decay term −(V − Vrest) pulls the voltage back toward rest in the absence of input, just as real ion channel conductances do.

SymbolDefaultMeaning
τm10 msMembrane time constant — how fast V decays toward Vrest without input
Vrest−70 mVResting (equilibrium) potential
Vreset−75 mVPost-spike reset potential (brief hyperpolarisation)
Vthr(t)−55 mVSpike threshold — enter a function of t to model dynamic thresholds
I(t)1.5 nAInput current — injected current or net synaptic drive
R(t)10 MΩMembrane resistance — higher R means more voltage change per unit of current

Despite its simplicity, the LIF model reproduces firing-rate statistics of real neurons with surprising accuracy and remains the standard building block for large-scale network simulations. The parameter functions I(t), Vthr(t), and R(t) let you explore how time-varying inputs and biological modulation shape neural activity — use the presets above as starting points.
